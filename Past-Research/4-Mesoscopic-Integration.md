# Mesoscopic Integration in the Sensory Cortex

Modelling of the “apparent motion” illusion in the visual cortex  
[Zerlaut et al., _J. Comput. Neurosci._ (2017)](https://drive.google.com/file/d/1V3r2XS9gTY_NPh2wTd2t2qaOyNN5WJUv/view?usp=share_link) ( & [Chemla et al., _J. Neurosci._ (2019)](https://drive.google.com/file/d/1siyxrKMRYJFUpYdFETW0W6ne_O8qhLKz/view?usp=share_link) )

In two companion papers with the team of F. Chavane (Univ. Marseille), we have investigated the neurophysiological basis of the "apparent motion" illusion by combining network dynamics modelling and experimental characterisation in monkey visual cortex. This visual illusion appears when two stimuli are flashed close enough in space and time: one perceives a unique moving stimulus.

In the first study (Zerlaut et al., _J. Comput. Neurosci._ 2017), I derived a model of cortical dynamics at the mesoscopic scale (1-10mm) by using a mean-field approach. We show that this model exhibits waves of stimulus-evoked activity propagating laterally. I calibrated the model parameters on VSD imaging measurements in the monkey visual cortex. Next, in the second study (J. Neurosci.), we report an important emergent property of the model: the activity evoked by two stimuli gives rise to a suppression phenomenon due to the sub-linear nature of synaptic integration in the network. The combination of lateral propagation and suppression in the model explains that two close stimuli lead to a smooth and moving cortical activity pattern in the visual cortex analogous to the neural representation of a single moving stimulus. We propose that this model offers a theoretical mechanism for the "illusory motion" phenomenon.
